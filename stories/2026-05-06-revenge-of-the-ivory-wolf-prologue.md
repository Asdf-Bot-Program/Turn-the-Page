The Den of Sugar Falls sat quietly beneath a grey and indecisive sky, the kind of sky that promises nothing and therefore, the stress of the day is nonexistent. After the particular unpleasantness that had surrounded Caedis, the group had earned their idleness. Bayard, Iris, Wylarin, Mia, and Volg moved through the estate the way water moves in a still pool, present, unhurried, content to simply be. It is worth noting, when observing such moments, how deceptive stillness can be. Still water has no idea what stone is about to break its surface. The first stone arrived with all the grace of something that had run out of options. A Khajiit came over the waterfall tumbling, suddenly, and entirely without dignity. Her name was Rali, though the rocks at the bottom of the waterfall are uncaring to such things. It was Volg and Mia who heard it first, the particular sound of something going wrong near water. It was they who stepped outside to find her crumpled on the bank, unconscious and worse for wear. They brought her inside without ceremony, to tend to her wounds and figure out exactly what was going on with this mysterious Khajit. Not far away, unconnected and yet about to become very connected, a Kahjit woman named Liliana walked into Sugar Falls looking for someone who was not there yet. She settled near the fire with the patience of someone who had been searching long enough to know that rest was not unwelcome. She watched Iris and Wylarin dance and let herself be still for just a moment. As she did the meat, she put on the fire crackled to perfection as she wondered about the spices that could be used to enhance its flavours. Suddenly, a portal announced itself with all the self-importance of magic that has decided it would like an audience. Through it stepped a Dunmer who carried the particular energy of someone who had heard rumours about a place and arrived to determine whether the rumours were worth believing. His name was Mevanos, and he had the look of a man whose stone-cold appearance could kill. What Mevanos had not considered, or perhaps had simply not noticed, was that he had brought a passenger. From behind him emerged a figure best described as an old woman that one could best describe as a Reach Witch, but not quite. Granny Lexy appeared behind him as though she had always been there, and the portal had simply formalised the arrangement.
Meanwhile, in the other wing of the estate, Bayard had heard of the commotion surrounding the Khajiit and went to see about it. Mia and Volg had gone to collect Iris and Wylarin because wounds, like most problems, improve with more hands, plus they both have had a history with helping those with troubled pasts, leaving Bayard to evaluate the situation alone. He healed what he could, which was considerable, and Rali regained enough of herself to immediately demonstrate how she had survived by leaping away from him in pure terror. Mia heard this and moved quickly, arriving with the calm efficiency of someone who has dealt with panic before and pulling from the quieter parts of her vampiric nature, smoothed out the sharp edges of Rali's fear. It worked; it also, as it turned out, was precisely the kind of help that helps maybe a bit too much. Iris felt the panic before she arrived at it, and when she came through the door with Wylarin, Mevanos, and Granny Lexy trailing behind her, she took in the scene with a particular expression. She had opinions about what Mia had done; she voiced them, concerned about the magical effect her vampiric nature could cause. Volg, for his part, had stayed behind. Not from disinterest but from the awareness of someone who knew his limitations for situations such as these. Liliana was near the fire, and Volg was the kind of creature who notices other creatures of his particular nature the way iron notices a magnet. They assessed each other with the silent language of two people who understand that the line between ally and threat can be drawn very quickly. They reached somewhat of an understanding as they walked over to join the rest. Iris set about the work of genuine calming, with the precision of someone paying attention that the architecture of Rali's fear had a specific load-bearing wall in it: Mevanos. Iris asked him, with the careful politeness of someone making a request that was not entirely a request, to wait outside. The moment Liliana saw Mevanos walk outside properly, she asked if he recognised her; it was clear he did. Nearly twenty years is a long time to go without seeing someone. It is also, as it happens, long enough for a punch to the chest to feel entirely deserved. She furiously explained that the punch was for abandoning them all those years ago. Liliana delivered it and then, without waiting for him to recover, pulled him into an embrace that said more than the punch had. Some reunions are complicated; this one wore its complications openly. They knew each other; that much was obvious. The specifics of how and why belonged to a longer story that the afternoon was not yet ready to tell. Hours settled around the group the way hours do when people are cautiously becoming acquainted, a little at a time. Names were exchanged, small truths were offered, the kind of people share when they are deciding how much of themselves to trust a stranger with. Rali was improving in the specific way that good food and decent healing tend to produce, fortified by the cake that Mia had made and the combined attention of Wylarin and Bayard. She was still watchful, but she was present, which was progress. Suddenly, the overcast very quickly evolved into a full-on storm. The storm that rolled in came with the weight of something that had been building since before anyone thought to check the horizon, and with it came two things riding the same wind but belonging to entirely different worlds. The first was the residual shadow of the Planemeld, that old wound in the fabric of things that Solstice still carried in its bones like an ache that never fully resolved. It arrived high into the sky with a hue of purple and blue that seemed to dance its way across the sky. Would have been considered beautiful if not for the implications of its creation. The second was something older and stranger and harder to name: it showed itself as a gust of wind that was not quite just wind, carrying leaves, grass, and the suggestion of intent as it moved through the entirety of the Sugar Falls estate with a hurried rush of something panicked. Then, almost like it finally noticed them, it stopped in front of the group. The leaves and grass gathered themselves to the floor with the slow deliberateness of a thought becoming a sentence. The wind shaped itself, and there, assembled from borrowed material and the particular desperation of someone who has tried every other option, stood the visage of an Argonian. It quickly became clear that he was panicking, and when he finally noticed them, he was surprised. Both of those things became true simply because his plan had actually worked when he had not expected it to. He introduced himself and said that his name was Vos-Greel. He explained that he was currently indisposed inside a manor on Solstice and had sent this message the way one throws something into the dark, hoping it lands somewhere useful, on the slim possibility that someone was listening. He then asked who they were and where they were located, trying to gauge just who exactly he contacted. The group, being reasonable people with appropriate caution, hesitated. Unfortunately for everyone else, Granny Lexy did not hesitate. She listed everyone's names with the cheerful completeness of someone who believed information is meant to be shared, mentioned that they were in Reaper's March, and finished before anyone had quite managed to stop her. Vos absorbed this with the gratitude of a man who has been alone long enough to find information from a stranger deeply welcome. He asked for their help as he explained his situation: a manor and an inability to leave. He thought he had a way out, he believed, but it required people who were not stuck to help him find it. Bayard and Wylarin were willing to help on the basis of simple decency. Volg, however, was willing on the basis of compensation. The stumble that followed, in which Vos searched for what he could actually offer them, resolved into a free bathhouse trip for all of them. This landed with Iris and Rali almost immediately. Then Vos, apparently deciding that he should give everything he had to offer, added that the manor was of House Corelanya and contained rare artifacts that could be of value. Mevanos immediately looked up as the group deliberated. Rali, during this deliberation, located what she apparently believed was a weapon in the form of a broom, which she did not immediately correct when she noticed it.
Once there, Corelanya Manor arrived in the way that places with reputations tend to have; before you could see it properly, you could feel it. The fog was specific, the stillness was specific; this was not the ordinary absence of wind and sound but the presence of something that had decided, long ago, that they preferred to stay away. Vos was in the doorway as he waved at the group, hoping that they would notice him. As they met up, he started to explain his situation, and he then offered to show them what would happen when he tried to leave the manor. The demonstration of the curse had a quality that unsettles the mind more than straightforward danger does, not pain or threat but wrongness. He tried to walk through the door and then, as though he had never left the doorway, was immediately back in the house, back facing the group. The group asked the reasonable question about whether they, too, would be trapped. Fortunately, Vos had done his research, gathered documents and cross-referenced them with the particular fervour of a man with nothing else to do, and the answer was firmly no. The curse was specific; it reached for those who were of House Corelanya. Then the question of why an Argonian from the Stone-Nest tribes had triggered it at all remained suspended in the air like smoke, too strange to press immediately. Once deliberation was settled, he invited them all inside. The inside of the manor held its breath; dust moved as it had somewhere to be. The halls held the specific weight of a place where knowledge had once been accumulated and then stopped accumulating abruptly. Vos had gathered what he could. He spread his documents across a table and explained what he had found: Valendir, the one who had placed the curse, was capable of granting exceptions. He was, presumably, still here in some form. The obstacle was location; Vos had searched the manor thoroughly and found no sign of him. The one place he hadn't checked was the gardens. Once outside, a plan started to form around the map Vos produced. Meanwhile, Volg and Liliana found the edges of the conversation and used them, the way two people who share a particular nature sometimes do. They discussed what they were, what they owed each other by virtue, and what would happen if either of them forgot themselves. They reached the same understanding they had reached before, now more formally. It was a practical conversation between two people who respected practicality. Behind the group, Mia and Iris slowed. The distance between them had been accumulating for long enough that walking into a haunted manor was apparently the occasion that felt right to address a lingering issue. Mia opened something she had been keeping closed, not words exactly, but the shape of what words would have to describe and let Iris feel the edges of it. The tragedy that a person carries changes how they look from the inside, including her entire village being slaughtered, and the death of her child. Understanding that requires proximity to that interior. Almost tearing up, Iris gained a newfound understanding of what some of the things Mia had been through. While all this happened, everyone followed Vos into the gardens, moving together toward something that had been waiting for long enough. The gardens of Corelanya Manor had the particular quality of a place that had been left to its own devices for long enough that it had developed opinions. The hedges grew with the confidence of things that answered to no one, and whatever had once been an orderly arrangement of paths had long since become a suggestion rather than a fact. The group moved through it the way people move through somewhere they have been told is dangerous, carefully. The skeletons and ghosts that called the gardens home were not dramatic about it. They proceeded in the functional way that combat sometimes does when the people involved know what they are doing. However, this time the walls reached out, three hands, bony and sudden, extended from the dense green and closed around Bayard, Iris, and Rali with the unhurried certainty of something that had been waiting for exactly this, then they were simply elsewhere. Not gone, Mia confirmed that much, reaching across using her vampiric sense, she sensed out and found them present and intact in another part of the manor. Volg issued the warning to the rest, with the economy of someone more interested in solving the problem than describing it, as the group pressed on. The dead end arrived as dead ends do, without apology, and what it contained was, ironically enough, a rat. The rat sat with the exhausted patience of someone who has been watching a situation deteriorate for longer than is reasonable and has finally decided to say something about it. It opened its mouth and told them, in a voice that carried the specific weariness of custodial disappointment, to stop making a mess of its gardens. They questioned the rat, as one does and as they do, a small orb separated itself from the creature and assembled, with the unhurried dignity of someone who has been a ghost long enough to know there is no particular rush, into the form of Valendir. The owner of the manor and the author of the curse. A man who had, apparently, been watching them as a rat, and had opinions about what he had seen. They explained the situation, Vos-Greel, stuck, not by choice, in need of an exception to the terms Valendir himself had written. Valendir brought Vos forward and examined him with the attention of a man reading an important document. The curse was on him; that much was obvious, but the blood was not Corelanya. He stood with this contradiction for a moment, the way a scholar stands with a calculation that refuses to resolve correctly. He was confused while also, despite himself, intrigued. He agreed to help, as people sometimes do when something doesn't make sense and the only way to understand it better is to stay involved. The price was a choice: the bushes in the garden, trimmed by hand, today, or the Worm Cultists that had apparently made themselves comfortable in the lower levels of the manor, in the crypt. The group looked at the state of the gardens. They chose the Worm Cultists. Vos, it turned out, would not be joining them. The crypt required going outside, and outside remained outside his reach. He accepted this with the particular resignation of someone who has spent enough time trapped to have made peace with individual disappointments. Granny Lexy elected to stay with him, which she presented as companionship and which Valendir experienced as something else entirely. Once inside the crypt greeted them with the standard of somewhere that had been neglected in one direction and overcrowded in another. Summoned things moved through the dark with the purpose of creatures that had been told to be there. Then, further in, something unexpected: Worm Cultists on the ground, and the reason they were on the ground still very much in motion. Lauren moved through the space with the focused efficiency of someone who had come here for a specific reason and was not interested in anything that interfered with it. The kind of competence that doesn't announce itself, and when the last of them were dealt with, she turned, and the sword and shield turned with her, pointed at the group with the same efficiency she had just applied to everything else. What followed was the particular challenge of a conversation where one party has words, and the other has hands, and the two are not immediately compatible. Lauren was mute; she communicated in sign language with the calm of someone for whom this was simply how things worked. Mia, Wylarin, and Mevanos understood her. The rest understood Mia, who translated with the matter-of-fact steadiness of someone more interested in the content of what was being said than the novelty of how it was being said. What Lauren was saying was this: she was here for something that might help her reclaim a connection to magic she had lost years ago. The how of that loss was hers to keep for now. They explained their own reasons for being there, and when the explanations satisfied each other sufficiently, they continued. The leader of this particular pocket of Worm Cult had the energy of a man who had constructed a situation he was proud of and was not prepared to have it dismantled. The room he occupied held two things worth noting: a large tree climbing toward the ceiling with a strange blue crack of light snaking through it like a frozen lightning strike, and several papers resting on a nearby empty grave. He cast a spell before the fight properly began. The spell filled the room around him and set itself to work; whatever damage came in was neutralized, healed as if it never happened. As the fight went on, it was Wylarin and Lauren who found the answer, the way two people sometimes find an answer when they are both looking at the same problem from useful angles. A rope and a shield were the answer, the geometry of where the spell worked and where it stopped working. Wylarin got the rope around him; meanwhile, Lauren took the other end, attached it to her shield, and threw the shield with precision. It got caught in the nearby catwalk, and the rope went taut. The Worm Cult Leader went with it, dangling, no longer within the comfortable range of his own protection. The group proceeded, as agreed, to address this by beating the everliving shit out of him. Mevanos finished it with fire that was so bright it might as well have been day. The robes went first, and his dignity went with them. The Worm Cult Leader looked down at himself, assessed the situation, made a decision and teleported away. Alone in the room, the group turned their attention to the tree. The blue crack in its bark was not natural, which was apparent, and was not accidental, which took slightly longer to establish. The Worm Cultists had been using a weak point in the curse's architecture, something Valendir had either left incomplete. As well as a portal, pointed somewhere the group did not particularly want to go. Mevanos looked at it the way he looked at most things that could be taken apart and put back together differently. He worked at the spell until it pointed somewhere more useful, outside the manor and away from the curse. The group stood with this for a moment. They had done what Valendir had asked of them, but they also had this new option that would completely bypass him. They weighed both of their options. Valendir was an old necromancer from Clan Corelanya. There was a good possibility that he wouldn’t keep up his word. Plus, there was a matter of pride; why use his help when they could do it all on their own? However, on the other side, the ghost of an ancient necromancer was not someone that you would want to make upset. After much deliberation, the answer they arrived at was honesty to Valednir, with the alternate exit kept quietly in reserve, the way a sensible person keeps a second option, simply present. Granny Lexy was in the main hall when they returned, comfortable in the way that suggested she had been comfortable for some time. Vos, however, was not. She had mentioned that she last saw him in the way of the dining hall, so the group started there. Once inside, they saw him emerging from the direction of the underground laboratory, the Reanimatorium, at a speed that suggested urgency, pivoted when he saw them, and adopted the posture of someone who had been looking at the nearby statues this whole time and found them very interesting. Volg looked at him with distrust and asked what was going on. Vos offered the explanation that he is part of the Fighters Guild and finding the Voskrona Eyes was simply part of his job. The group received this explanation with the polite skepticism it warranted. Valendir materialized with the specific energy of someone who had been watching the entire time and had been wondering when to mention it. He registered his disappointment about the weak spot and the thoughts that had been considered as an escape option, but he granted the exception anyway. He also made sure to mention the three that had been separated from them once they entered the gardens: Bayard, Iris, and Rali. They had apparently had their own adventure of mayhem throughout the manor, and Valendir had had enough of them and wanted them out as well. A secret passageway opened up, and out walked the missing three, looking a tad bit more traumatized than before. However, despite that, Vos-Greel was finally able to walk through the door after all this time. The relief that came off him was the kind that has been compressed for long enough that it arrives all at once. He thanked them with the sincerity of someone who means it more than they know how to say. Then came the matter of compensation, as promised, one free day at the bathhouse was met with a wall of silent consensus that it was insufficient. Three days was met the same kind of silence; the negotiation moved through several positions with the particular momentum of a group that had just spent time in a crypt and had opinions about what that was worth. The wall held until Vos arrived at an offer that the group found, at last, proportionate: a free trip, any time, forever and with that the wall finally came down. As the group made their way back, Mevanos stopped walking and turned around. Some people leave a place and take what the experience gave them. Mevanos was the kind of person who went back in; he descended again into the Reanimatorium with the focused purpose of someone who knows what they are looking for and intends to find it. The same area that Vos was seen coming out of. When he emerged, he carried a scroll, a document that concerned itself with the movement and control of souls in ways that most institutions would consider beyond their scope. Soul transfer, not only that, but also soul domination. He had a pleasant conversation with Valendir on the way out and parted on reasonable terms. He went back to his tower, scroll in hand, with the expression of a man who considered the day well spent.
