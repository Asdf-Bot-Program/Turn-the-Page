backstory: >-
  Sigrun was born beneath a sky that never seemed to rest. Wind tore constantly
  across the jagged hills of the Reach, carrying with it the scent of ash, wet
  stone, and things older than memory. It was not a place for comfort, nor for
  softness. It was a place that shaped people into something harder or broke
  them entirely. Sigrun did not break. From the time she could stand, she was
  expected to endure. A blade was placed in her hand before she could properly
  grip it, and when she dropped it, she was told to pick it up again. Bruises
  came quickly. Lessons came quicker. In the Reach, pain was not cruelty; it was
  instruction. She learned well. Other children avoided her, not because she
  sought them out, but because she did not know when to stop. When a fight
  began, she saw it through. When she fell, she stood again. When she bled, she
  kept moving. There was something in hersomething that did not yield easily,
  and in the Reach that drew attention. Not kind attention. A clan witch began
  watching her. Quietly at first, from the edges of campfires and ritual
  circles, where shadows gathered and lingered longer than they should. The
  woman was old, though no one spoke of how old, and her presence carried the
  weight of things best left unasked. When she finally called Sigrun forward, it
  was not an offer. It was a decision already made. Training under the witch was
  nothing like learning to fight. There were no drills, no shouted corrections,
  only mistakes, and the consequences that followed them. Sigrun learned to
  carve runes into bone, to mix blood with ash, to speak words that felt wrong
  in her mouth long before they left it. She learned, most of all, how close the
  world truly was to something else. Something to watch. Something waiting. And
  among all the forces she brushed against, one drew her more than the rest,
  Fire. Not for its warmth. Not for its light, but for its certainty. Fire did
  not hesitate. There was no doubt. It is simply consumed. Sigrun understood
  that. The ritual came sooner than it should have. She knew it. Even then. But
  she did not step back. The circle had been carved deep into the stone, its
  edges blackened by earlier attempts that had not gone entirely as planned. The
  air around it felt heavy, as though the world itself were pressing inward,
  listening. The witch began the incantation. The words scraped against the
  inside of Sigrun’s mind more than her ears. The fire came slowly at first,
  flickers of heat, whispers of something forming just beyond sight. Then it
  surged. I was not willing to answer. The summoning did not fail. It resisted.
  The air tore open, not cleanly, but violently, like something forced through a
  space too small to contain it. Flame erupted outward, not shaped, not
  controlled, just a raw, furious presence. The witch vanished in an instant.
  There was no struggle. No final word. One moment, she stood. The next, she did
  not exist. Sigrun should have run. Any sane person would have. Instead, she
  stood her ground. The fire struck her. It should have burned her to nothing.
  Instead, it did something far worse. It went into her. There was a moment,
  brief, unbearable, where she felt herself come apart. Not die. Not fade. As
  though something inside her was being replaced by something that did not
  belong. Then, just as suddenly as it began, it stopped. Silence returned
  first. Then the cold. Sigrun looked down at herself, half expecting to find
  nothing left. But her hands were still there. Her breath still came. Her heart
  still beat. The changes did not arrive all at once. 


  They unfolded slowly, as if whatever had entered her was still deciding what she would become. Her skin darkened, subtle at first, then more pronounced. Fine, fractured lines appeared beneath the surface, like cracks in cooling stone, faintly glowing when the light was low. Her eyes lost their reflection, holding a steady, unnatural warmth instead. Even standing still, she radiated heat not enough to burn, but enough that others noticed. Enough that they kept their distance. The clan gathered, as they always did when something changed. They spoke in quiet tones, watching her as one might watch a storm forming on the horizon. Some called her chosen. Others called her unfinished. A few simply called her dangerous. Sigrun did not wait to hear what they would decide; she left. The world beyond the Reach was different, but no kinder. She found work where she had always worked through her strength. Steel returned to her hands, heavier and more reliable than the uncertain forces she had brushed against. It made sense in a way magic never had. But the fire remained. At first, it was nothing more than a presence of low, constant heat beneath her ribs. Then it began to answer her. Not like a tool. Like a reaction. Anger fed it. Pain sharpened it. In battle, it bled into her strikes, turning effort into impact, impact into something harder to stop. The more she pushed, the more it rose to meet her. It did not obey. It grew. The first dragon was not a hunt. It was a misfortune. A shadow passed overhead. Then another. Then the sound was deep, resonant, wrong in a way nothing else was. People ran. Sigrun did not. The dragon descended in a rush of wings and heat, its presence pressing down on the world like a weight. It saw her standing there, still, unflinching, and for a moment, it seemed to pause. Then it breathed. The fire was unlike anything she had known. This was not the flicker within her. Not the wild surge of the ritual. This was something ancient. Something absolute. It struck her. Pain came all at once, blinding, overwhelming. Her body gave out beneath it, knees slamming into the ground as the heat tore through armor, through flesh, through everything she was. She should have died there. She didn’t. Something inside her rose not to fight the flame, not to control it, but to endure it. Two fires met within her, neither willing to yield. When the dragon’s breath faded, she was still there. Burned. Broken. Breathing. The dragon did not stay. It did not need to. Sigrun lay in the ash long after it had gone, staring up at the sky it had torn apart. Then, slowly, painfully, she laughed. Not because it was funny. Because she understood. She could survive this. Not cleanly. Not without cost. Not forever. But longer than most. And sometimes, longer is enough. Since then, she has followed the signs others avoid. Rumors of wings. Burned villages. Shadows crossing the sky where none should be. She does not call herself a hunter. She does not claim victory. But when the fire comes, she does not run. She walks into it. The cracks in her skin burn brighter now. Her breath carries heat even in the coldest air. And there are moments, dangerous when she no longer feels like something carrying fire……but something containing it. The ritual did not fail. It is still unfolding. And one day, Sigrun Fire-Fueled will not walk into flame; she will become one.
